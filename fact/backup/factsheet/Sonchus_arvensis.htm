Sonchus arvensis

Family

Asteraceae

Common Name

Perennial sow thistle

Regulation

  • Primary Noxious, Class 2 in the Canadian Weed Seeds Order (2016) under the Seeds Act.

Distribution

Canadian: Occurs in all provinces and territories (Brouillet et al. 2010+, CFIA and NRCan/CFS 2011+).

Worldwide: Native to Europe and temperate Asia, as far as the Russian Far East. Introduced in North America, Argentina, Chile, Australia, New Zealand, Japan, Indonesia and the Philippines (USDA-ARS 2016). In the United States it occurs throughout except for a few southern states (Kartesz 2011).

Duration of Life Cycle

Perennial

Seed/Fruit Type

Achene

Seed Identification Features

Average Seed Size

  • Length: 2.5 mm
  • Width:1.0 mm

Seed Shape

  • Oval shaped; flattened

Seed Surface Texture

  • Dull surface with longitudinal folds

Seed Colour

  • Dark brown

Other Seed Features

  • Pappus of fine white filaments 2-3 times as long as the seed may be present.

Habitat and Crop Association

Cultivated fields, pastures, meadows, lawns, gardens, fences, shores, ditches, thickets, forests, roadsides and disturbed areas (Lemna and Messersmith 1990, Darbyshire 2003). A weed of any annual crop in temperate areas, notably cereals, corn, oilseed crops, sugarbeet, potatoes and vegetables (CABI 2016).

General Information

Perennial sow thistle was probably introduced into North America as a seed contaminant (Lemna and Messersmith 1990).

A single plant may produce 3000-9750 seeds (Lemna and Messersmith 1990), which can remain viable in the soil for several years (CABI 2016).

Similar species: Annual sow thistle (Sonchus oleraceus) achenes

Similar species: Annual sow thistle (Sonchus oleraceus) achene

Similar Species

Annual sow thistle (Sonchus oleraceus)

Annual sow thistle achenes are a similar size, oblong shape, strongly flattened and a surface with longitudinal ribs as perennial sow thistle.

Annual sow thistle achenes are a lighter colour with shallow surface wrinkling and not longitudinally folded as perennial sow thistle.
