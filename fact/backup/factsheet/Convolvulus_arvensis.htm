Convolvulus arvensis

Family

Convolvulaceae

Common Name

Field bindweed

Regulation

  • Primary Noxious, Class 2 in the Canadian Weed Seeds Order (2016) under the Seeds Act.

Distribution

Canadian: Occurs in BC, AB, SK, MB, ON, QC, NB, NS and PE (Brouillet et al. 2010+, CFIA and NRCan/CFS 2011+).

Worldwide: Native to northern Africa, Europe and Asia, where its distribution extends north to Siberia and south to Pakistan and Nepal (USDA-ARS 2016). Introduced to North and South America, Australia and New Zealand (CABI 2016). In the United States, it is most common in the Midwest and western regions (Kartesz 2011).

Duration of Life Cycle

Perennial

Seed/Fruit Type

Seed

Seed Identification Features

Average Seed Size

  • Length: 3.0 - 4.5 mm
  • Width: 2.3 - 3.5 mm

Seed Shape

  • Ovate, three-angled with the hilum at the narrow end

Seed Surface Texture

  • Coarsely roughened with dense tubercles

Seed Colour

  • Greyish-brown, brown, orange to black with lighter tubercles

Other Seed Features

  • Hilum is orange and oval-shaped.

Habitat and Crop Association

Cultivated fields, old fields, pastures, gardens, fencelines, roadsides and other disturbed areas (Darbyshire 2003). A weed of 32 different crops in 44 countries, including wheat, barley, corn, legumes, sugarbeet, vegetables, cotton, tobacco, potato, pineapples, flax, alfalfa, vineyards, and tree crops (Royer and Dickinson 1999, Weaver and Riley 1982, CABI 2016).

General Information

Field bindweed was introduced from Europe in the 18th century. It can be dispersed as a contaminant of crop seeds (Ministry of Agriculture, Food, and Fisheries 2002).

Field bindweed grows best in fertile, well drained soils, in regions with moderate to good rainfall (CABI 2016). Seed production ranges from 25 to 300 seeds per plant, depending on conditions. Seeds remain viable in the soil for over 20 years (Ministry of Agriculture, Food, and Fisheries 2002).

Similar Species

Hedge bindweed (Calystegia sepium)

Hedge bindweed has a similar obovate shape, brownish colour, orange hilum and surface tubercles.

Hedge bindweed is larger by 0.5 - 1.0 mm, has a large hump on the convex side, smaller and less dense tubercles and the hilum is D-shaped rather than oval in field bindweed.
