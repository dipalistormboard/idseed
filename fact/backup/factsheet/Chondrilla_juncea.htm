Chondrilla juncea

Family

Asteraceae

Common Name

Rush skeletonweed

Regulation

  • Primary Noxious, Class 2 in the Canadian Weed Seeds Order (2016) under the Seeds Act.

Distribution

Canadian:Occurs in BC and ON (Brouillet et al. 2010+, CFIA and NRCan/CFS 2011+).

Worldwide: Native to northern Africa, temperate Asia and Europe. Introduced to North America, Argentina, Latvia, Australia and New Zealand (USDA-ARS 2016). Infests millions of acres in Washington, Idaho, Oregon and California (FNA 1993+).

Duration of Life Cycle

Perennial

Seed/Fruit Type

Achene

Seed Identification Features

Average Seed Size

  • Achene length (no beak): 3.0 - 4.0 mm
  • Width: 0.5 - 1.0 mm
  • Length of beak: 5.0 - 6.0 mm

Seed Shape

  • Oblong, tapering at the base, compressed

Seed Surface Texture

  • Woody, dull, small spines at the top of the achene

Seed Colour

  • Yellow-brown

Other Seed Features

  • A style peg extends from the top that is usually broken in samples.
  • Series of spines around the top of the achene.

Habitat and Crop Association

Cultivated fields, pastures, rangelands, forests, roadsides and disturbed areas (Darbyshire 2003). A serious weed of wheat in Australia (FNA 1993+).

General Information

Rush skeletonweed was first reported in the United States near Spokane, Washington in 1938 (Shelly 1994), and is now found in the northern regions and California (Kartesz 2011). Rush skeletonweed is often found on well drained, light soils (Shaw et al. 2008).

One plant may produce as many as 15,000–20,000 seeds (Ministry of Agriculture, Food, and Fisheries 2002). Seeds are dispersed by wind and animals over considerable distances (Shaw et al. 2008).

Similar species: Dandelion (Taraxacum officinale) achenes

Similar Species

Dandelion (Taraxacum officinale)

Dandelion has a similar oblong shape, yellow colour and top peg as rush skeletonweed.

Dandelion is shorter, wider, a darker colour and the top spines extend further down the fruit than on rush skeletonweed achenes.
