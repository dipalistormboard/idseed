Mayweed (Anthemis cotula ) achenes

Mayweed (Anthemis cotula ) achene

Mayweed (Anthemis cotula) achene, close-up of surface texture

Anthemis cotula

Family

Asteraceae

Common Name

Mayweed

Regulation

  • Secondary Noxious, Class 3 in the Canadian Weed Seeds Order (2016) under the Seeds Act.

Distribution

Canadian: Occurs across Canada except in NT and NU (Brouillet et al. 2010+, CFIA and NRCan/CFS 2011+), although reports from SK and PE are questionable (Brouillet et al. 2010+).

Worldwide: Native to the Mediterranean region and widely introduced elsewhere including South Africa, Australia, New Zealand, parts of Europe, and North and South America (CABI 2016, USDA-ARS 2016). It is found throughout the United States, including Alaska and Hawaii (Kartesz 2011, USDA-NRCS 2016).

Duration of Life Cycle

Annual

Seed/Fruit Type

Achene

Seed Identification Features

Average Seed Size

  • Length: 1.0 - 1.5 mm
  • Width: 0.7 - 1.0 mm

Seed Shape

  • Oblong, tapering to the base

Seed Surface Texture

  • Warty in longitudinal rows

Seed Colour

  • Straw yellow to brown

Other Seed Features

  • Surface is corky and easily scratched.

Habitat and Crop Association

Cultivated fields, pastures, gardens, lawns, railway lines, roadsides and disturbed areas (FNA 1993+, Darbyshire 2003, CABI 2016). Found in most annual and many perennial crops, and most abundant in cereals and legumes (CABI 2016). Particularly adapted to rich, wet soils and may be problematic in conservation tillage systems (CABI 2016).

General Information

Mayweed seeds are most commonly spread by agricultural practices, and early infestations in cereal crops were thought to be largely via contaminated seed. Seeds may also be spread on agricultural equipment, and in hay, bedding, and animal manure.

Other accidental introductions are attributed to ship's ballast, and in some areas mayweed may have been introduced for medicinal or ornamental purposes.

Individual plants may produce anywhere from 550-27,000 seeds depending on environmental conditions, and seed may remain viable for up to 25 years (CABI 2016).

Similar species: Corn chamomile (Anthemis arvensis) achenes

Similar Species

Corn chamomile (Anthemis arvensis)

The achenes of corn chamomile have a similar wedge shape, straw yellow colour and corky surface as mayweed.

Corn chamomile achenes are larger by up to 1.0 mm long and 0.5 mm wide than mayweed, and the surface is ribbed rather than warty.
