Barbarea spp.

Family

Brassicaceae

Common Name

Yellow rocket (Winter cress)

Regulation

  • Secondary Noxious, Class 3 in the Canadian Weed Seeds Order (2016) under the Seeds Act.

Distribution

Canadian: The genus includes 4 species found in Canada, distributed across all provinces and territories (Brouillet et al. 2010+, CFIA and NRCan/CFS 2011+). The native species B. orthoceras occurs across Canada except NS, while the introduced species occur as follows: B. stricta in ON and QC; B. verna in BC and NL, and; B. vulgaris in all provinces but not the territories.

Worldwide: The genus contains about 20 species with the majority distributed in the temperate regions of Eurasia and North America, and a few species in subtropical regions of East Asia and Australia (Agerbirk et al. 2003). The type species, B. vulgaris, has a wide native distribution area in Eurasia, and is introduced as a noxious weed in North America, Africa, Australia and New Zealand (MacDonald and Cavers 1991, Agerbirk et al. 2003).

Duration of Life Cycle

Biennial to short-lived perennial

Seed/Fruit Type

Seed

Seed Identification Features

Average Seed Size

Barbarea verna

  • Length: 2.0 - 2.5 mm
  • Width: 1.0 -1.5 mm

Barbarea vulgaris

  • Length: 1.5 mm
  • Width: 1.0 mm

Seed Shape

  • Oval to oblong, compressed

Seed Surface Texture

  • Seeds are covered in a waxy, translucent, yellowish film
  • Surface is strongly reticulate
  • B. verna is larger than B vulgaris and has more prominent dark-brown to grey reticulations.

Seed Colour

  • Dark-greyish brown and overlaid by yellowish film

Other Seed Features

  • Hilum is in a notch at one end of the seed.

  • A groove extends part of the way along the length of the seed, separating the cotyledons from the seed root.

Habitat and Crop Association

Cultivated fields, gardens, pastures, meadows, old fields, shores and swamps, roadsides and disturbed areas (Darbyshire 2003). B. vulgaris is an important weed of small-seeded grain and hay crops in Canada, legume-grass meadows in the U.S., and vegetable and fruit crops in Europe (MacDonald and Cavers 1991).

General Information

Yellow rocket produces abundant seed early in the growing season and behaves as an early spring dominant. Seeds may be spread as contaminants in seed lots of timothy and other similar sized grains; they may also be spread in hay and manure, and have been shown to remain viable after passing through a variety of animals.

Individual plants may produce up to 88,000 seeds per plant, and seeds may remain dormant in the soil for 10-20 years. It can also reproduce vegetatively from the roots and rosettes (MacDonald and Cavers 1991).

Similar Species

Common scurvygrass (Cochlearia officinalis)

Common scurvygrass seeds are a similar size, oval-oblong shape, strong reticulate pattern, distinctive groove and hilum position (in a notch at one end of the seed) as yellow rocket.

Common scurvygrass seeds are a reddish colour with the seed root extending past the hilum notch. The seed root of yellow rocket is even with the hilum notch.
