Sorghum halepense

Family

Poaceae

Common Name

Johnson grass

Regulation

  • Primary Noxious, Class 2 in the Canadian Weed Seeds Order (2016) under the Seeds Act.

Distribution

Canadian: Present in ON (Brouillet et al. 2010+, CFIA and NRCan/CFS 2011+).

Worldwide: Native range uncertain, possibly native to northern Africa and western and central Asia as far as India (Warwick and Black 1983, USDA-ARS 2016). Widely introduced elsewhere, including eastern Asia, North and South America, Europe, southern Africa, Australia and New Zealand (CABI 2016).

Duration of Life Cycle

Perennial

Seed/Fruit Type

Spikelet

Seed Identification Features

Average Seed Size

  • Length: 3.5 mm
  • Width: 1.7 mm

Seed Shape

  • Oval with two stalks at the base

Seed Surface Texture

  • Glumes are smooth and shiny with teeth near the top

Seed Colour

  • Straw yellow at the tips of the glumes darkening to a reddish-purple at the base. The pedicels are straw yellow.

Other Seed Features

  • Papery, inert spikelets may be attached to the pedicels.
  • Can have light hairs around the pedicels and the base of the spikelet.
  • The grain is reddish-coloured with a large embryo.

Habitat and Crop Association

Cultivated fields, pastures, orchards, roadsides and disturbed areas (Darbyshire 2003, CABI 2016). A serious weed of many crops, notably cereals, corn, sorghum, soybeans, beans, cotton, rice, sugarcane, citrus and grapevine (CABI 2016).

General Information

Johnson grass was introduced into the southeastern United States as a forage crop in the early 19th century and then prompted weed control measures in the early 20th century (Warwick & Black 1983).

Johnson grass produces abundant seed (up to 28,000 seeds per plant) which are readily shed when mature and can be carried by wind or rain water (CABI 2016). Seeds can remain viable in the soil for at least 7 years (CABI 2016). They also retain their viability after passing through the gut of animals such as birds or cattle (Warwick and Black 1983).

Similar Species

Sudan grass (Sorghum bicolor subsp. drummondii)

The spikelets of Sudan grass have a similar dark color, oval shape, hard glumes and pedicels as Johnson grass.

Sudan grass spikelets are up to 2.0 mm wider than Johnson grass, and tend to have a darker colour on the glumes without the straw yellow tips of Johnson grass. The pedicels of Sudan grass also do not have as well-developed cups at the ends as Johnson grass does.
