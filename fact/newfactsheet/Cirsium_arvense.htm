Canada thistle (Cirsium arvense) achenes

Canada thistle (Cirsium arvense) achene, top view

Canada thistle (Cirsium arvense) deformed achenes

Cirsium arvense

Family

Asteraceae

Common Name

Canada thistle

Regulation

  • Primary Noxious, Class 2 in the Canadian Weed Seeds Order (2016) under the Seeds Act.

Distribution

Canadian: Occurs in all provinces and territories except NU (Brouillet et al. 2010+, CFIA and NRCan/CFS 2011+).

Worldwide: Native to most of Europe and temperate Asia to as far south as India and Nepal. Introduced to North America, Argentina, Chile, Iceland, Greenland, South Africa, Australia and New Zealand (USDA-ARS 2016).

Duration of Life Cycle

Perennial

Seed/Fruit Type

Achene

Seed Identification Features

Average Seed Size

  • Length: 2.5 - 4.0 mm
  • Width: 1.0 mm

Seed Shape

  • Elongate oval; straight to slightly curved, tapering at the base

Seed Surface Texture

  • Fibrous and woody texture, thin longitudinal furrows can be seen with magnification

Seed Colour

  • Dull yellow to brown

Other Seed Features

  • Narrow pale collar (0.1 - 0.2 mm) at the top of the achene.
  • Immature fruits can have plumose pappus hairs.

Habitat and Crop Association

Cultivated fields, old fields, gardens, lawns, pastures, fencelines, shores, thickets, open forests, roadsides, railway lines and other disturbed areas (Darbyshire 2003). Infests crops including wheat, corn, peas, beans, sugarbeets and potatoes, as well as pastures and rangelands (Moore 1975).

General Information

Seeds of Canada thistle were likely introduced as a contaminant of crop seed in the late 18th century (Moore 1975). Canada thistle grows over a wide range of habitats and elevations, preferring rich loam soils (Ministry of Agriculture, Food, and Fisheries 2002).

Seed production averages 1,500 seeds per plant, but these remain viable for only a few years (Ministry of Agriculture, Food, and Fisheries 2002). It also produces deep creeping rhizomes that allow it to survive below normal tillage depths (Royer and Dickinson 1999).

Similar species: Nodding thistle (Carduus nutans) achenes

Similar species: Nodding thistle (Carduus nutans) achene

Similar Species

Nodding thistle (Carduus nutans)

Nodding thistle has a similar size, shape and colour as Canada thistle.

Nodding thistle seeds have a smooth, varnish-like surface texture with transverse markings, Canada thistle seed surface is fibrous and woody with thin furrows and no transverse lines.

The top peg is thicker and wider in nodding thistle. The immature achenes of nodding thistle has filamentous pappus hairs and Canada thistle has plumose hairs.
