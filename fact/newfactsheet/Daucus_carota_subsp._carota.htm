Daucus carota subsp. carota

Family

Apiaceae

Common Name

Wild carrot

Regulation

  • Secondary Noxious, Class 3 in the Canadian Weed Seeds Order (2016) under the Seeds Act.

Distribution

Canadian: Occurs in BC, MB, NB, NL, NS, ON, PE, QC, SK (Brouillet et al. 2010+, CFIA and NRCan/CFS 2011+).

Worldwide: Native to Europe, northern Africa and southwestern Asia (CABI 2016, USDA-ARS 2016). Widely introduced elsewhere in temperate regions including China, Japan, Australasia and North America (CABI 2016, USDA-ARS 2016).

Duration of Life Cycle

Biennial

Seed/Fruit Type

Schizocarp, divided into 2 mericarps

Seed Identification Features

Average Seed Size

  • Length: 2.0 - 4.0 mm
  • Width: 1.3 - 2.0 mm

Seed Shape

  • Oval to teardrop-shaped, flat on one side

Seed Surface Texture

  • Dull, with 5 ribs on outer side and 2 small arched ribs on the inner side

Seed Colour

  • Dull grey-brown with straw yellow ribs and bristles

Other Seed Features

  • Ribs are straight; the oil ducts between have a smooth surface.
  • Ribs are usually winged with prominent hairs that may be removed during processing.
  • Each rib is lined with small bristles that are visible even if the seed is worn.
  • One end has a pointed remnant floral style.

Habitat and Crop Association

Waste places, roadsides and disturbed areas, old fields, meadows and underused or depleted pastures (Dale 1974, Darbyshire 2003). Not typically considered a weed of cultivated fields on prime agricultural land but increasingly found in no-till production systems (Dale 1974, Cowbrough 2004). Reported in corn, soybeans and winter wheat in Ontario (Cowbrough 2004).

General Information

Wild carrot spread to North America through garden plantings and escaped to become a weed of roadsides and waste places (Darbyshire 2003, DiTomaso and Healy 2007). It may also have been introduced as a seed contaminant (CABI 2016).

Wild carrot hybridizes readily with cultivated carrot (DiTomaso and Healy 2007) and was first reported in Ontario and Quebec in 1883 (Royer and Dickinson 1999). Seeds are spread by wind, or by attachment to animals and clothing; they may also be spread in contaminated seed or grain (Dale 1974, CABI 2016). Individual plants produce 1000-40,000 seeds and seeds remain viable in the soil for several years (Dale 1974).

Similar Species

Poison hemlock (Conium maculatum)

Poison hemlock has similar teardrop shape, dull brown-coloured fruits and distinct ribs as wild carrot.

Poison hemlock may be up to 1.0 mm longer, is thicker and lacks the rib-associated hairs of wild carrot.

The seed surface of poison hemlock is wrinkled between the wavy ribs, and wild carrot is smooth between the straight ribs.
