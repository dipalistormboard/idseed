Centaurea solstitialis

Family

Asteraceae

Common Name

Yellow starthistle

Regulation

  • Prohibited Noxious, Class 1 in the Canadian Weed Seeds Order (2016) under the Seeds Act. All imported and domestic seed must be free of Prohibited Noxious weed seeds.
  • Listed on the List of Pests Regulated by Canada established under the Plant Protection Act. D-12-01 Phytosanitary requirements to prevent the introduction of plants regulated as pests in Canada.

Distribution

Canadian: Previously reported from AB, BC, ON and SK; however, no populations have persisted to date (CFIA 2014).

Worldwide: Native to northern Africa and eastern Europe across to central Asia. Introduced in other parts of Africa and Europe, as well as North and South America, Australia, and New Zealand (CABI 2016). Found in many U.S. states (USDA-NRCS 2016).

Duration of Life Cycle

Winter annual

Seed/Fruit Type

Achene

Seed Identification Features

Average Seed Size

  • Length: 2.5 mm

  • Width: 1.3 mm
  • Pappus length: 1.0 - 4.0 mm

Seed Shape

  • Oblong with rounded bottom and flat top, compressed; large notch near the base

Seed Surface Texture

  • Seeds from the centre of the flower head have a glossy surface; seeds from the outer edge have a dull surface.

Seed Colour

  • Most are straw yellow with a pale collar, base and a central stripe

  • Achenes from the outer edge are dark brown with reddish-brown and pale yellow streaks.

Other Seed Features

  • Seeds are strongly dimorphic: the seeds from the centre of the flower head are more numerous and have a pale, glossy surface and a long, white pappus.
  • The less numerous seeds from the outer edge of the flower head are dull and dark coloured with no pappus.

Habitat and Crop Association

Pastures, rangelands, grasslands, and roadsides in dry areas (CFIA 2014). Occasionally found as a weed of alfalfa and grain crops but mainly a weed of rangelands (CABI 2016).

General Information

Yellow starthistle was accidentally introduced into California during the Gold Rush era of the mid-1800s (DiTomaso and Healy 2007). The species is a problem in California with millions of hectares infested, and is spreading into Washington, Oregon and Idaho (CABI 2016).

The seeds are locally dispersed by the spiny seed heads caught in animal fur and farm machinery, and contaminated crop seed is a risk for introduction into new areas (Zouhar 2002).

Similar Species

Malta knapweed (Centaurea melitensis)

Malta knapweed achenes are a similar size, shape, and pale colour as the achenes of yellow star-thistle from the inside of the seed head.

Malta knapweed has a more prominent basal notch, a shorter pappus with wider bristles, and a grey background colour when compared to yellow star-thistle achenes.
