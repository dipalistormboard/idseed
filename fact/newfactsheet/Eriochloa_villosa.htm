Eriochloa villosa

Family

Poaceae

Common Name

Woolly cup grass

Regulation

  • Prohibited Noxious, Class 1 in the Canadian Weed Seeds Order (2016) under the Seeds Act. All imported and domestic seed must be free of Prohibited Noxious weed seeds.
  • Listed on the List of Pests Regulated by Canada established under the Plant Protection Act. D-12-01 Phytosanitary requirements to prevent the introduction of plants regulated as pests in Canada.

Distribution

Canadian: Limited populations are under official control in QC.

Worldwide: Native to temperate and subtropical parts of eastern Asia. Reported to be introduced beyond its native range in the Caucasus region, western Siberia, southern Asia and Iran. Also introduced to the Ukraine, France and the United States (Darbyshire et al. 2003). Well-established in the mid-western states (CFIA 2014).

Duration of Life Cycle

Annual

Seed/Fruit Type

Spikelet

Seed Identification Features

Average Seed Size

  • Length: 3.9 - 5.5 mm

  • Width: 2.0 - 2.5 mm

Seed Shape

  • Ovate with a pointed tip, compressed

Seed Surface Texture

  • Outer bracts are smooth and leathery, inner bracts are hard with a grid pattern

Seed Colour

  • Straw yellow, often with purple or streaks, inner bracts are straw yellow without streaks

Other Seed Features

  • Ring of hardened tissue at the base of the spikelet.

Habitat and Crop Association

Cultivated fields, open grassy places, hillsides, roadsides, along fences and ditches, and wastelands (CFIA 2012, 2014). A problematic weed in corn and soybeans in the United States (CFIA 2014).

General Information

Woolly cup grass was accidentally introduced into the United States in the 1940s (Owen 1990). In the midwestern United States, where it interferes with corn and soybean crops, it has proven difficult to control with pre-emergence herbicides because it germinates throughout the growing season (Owen 1990).

This species has also appeared in climatic zones outside of the Midwest in places such as California and Pennsylvania, suggesting that the species can adapt to many climatic conditions (Darbyshire et al. 2003).

Similar species: Field paspalum (Paspalum laeve) floret (L) and spikelet (R)

Similar Species

Field paspalum (Paspalum laeve)

The 2 species are a similar size, straw-yellow colour, oval shape, and flattened cross-section.

Field paspalum has a humped profile, papery outer bracts and no purple streaks, woolly cup grass is flattened in profile, leathery outer bracts and purple streaks. Field paspalum does not have a ring of tissue at the base.
