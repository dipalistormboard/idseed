Fact Sheets

Japanese brome (Bromus japonicus) florets

Japanese brome (Bromus japonicus) floret

Japanese brome (Bromus japonicus) florets

Japanese brome (Bromus japonicus) floret, palea teeth

Bromus japonicus

Family

Poaceae

Common Name

Japanese brome

Regulation

  • Secondary Noxious, Class 3 in the Canadian Weed Seeds Order (2016) under the Seeds Act.

Distribution

Canadian: Occurs in AB, BC, MB, NB, NS, ON, QC, SK, YT (Brouillet et al. 2010+, CFIA and NRCan/CFS 2011+).

Worldwide: Native to northern Africa and temperate Eurasia from France eastward to China, Japan and Korea (USDA-ARS 2016). Introduced in parts of Europe, Australia, New Zealand, South Africa and North and South America (Holm et al. 1991, USDA-ARS 2016). Occurs throughout most of the United States (Barkworth et al. 2007).

Duration of Life Cycle

Annual

Seed/Fruit Type

Floret

Seed Identification Features

Average Seed Size

  • Floret length: 7.0 - 9.0 mm
  • Floret width: 1.0 - 2.0 mm
  • Awn length: 8.0 - 13.0 mm

Seed Shape

  • Long oval-shaped with a flared top and extended sides

Seed Surface Texture

  • Lemma and palea are papery with hyaline margins, scabrous on lower 1/2

Seed Colour

  • Straw yellow

Other Seed Features

  • Palea teeth longer than 1.0 mm.
  • Awn bends away from the lemma.

Habitat and Crop Association

Cultivated fields, old fields, rangelands, pastures, hay fields, gardens, roadsides, industrial sites and disturbed areas (Darbyshire 2003, Barkworth et al. 2007). A weed of winter wheat, grass and alfalfa seed fields, pastures and ranges, and many other places (Finnerty and Klingman 1962). Prefers somewhat moister habitats than B. tectorum (Kirkland and Brenzil 2007).

General Information

Japanese brome can invade pastures and rangelands where it competes with native species, but can be used as an early-season forage (Howard 1994). It has been observed to increase in natural areas under a fire-suppression management regime (Howard 1994).

In Canada, annual bromes are often an indicator of poor range condition, and readily infest areas where native vegetation has been disturbed by overgrazing, fire, and cultivation (Kirkland and Brenzil 2007). Japanese brome occurs as a contaminant in seed and grain (Canadian Grain Commission (CGC) data).

Similar Species

Soft chess (Bromus hordeaceus)

Soft chess has a similar size, shape, colour and extended lemma sides.

The caryopsis is not pubescent on the margins.  The lemmas of soft chess are often pilose, but may be glabrous.  The awn is shorter, up to 4.0 mm.

Field brome (Bromus arvensis)

Field brome has a similar size, shape, colour and extended lemma sides.

Field brome's awn is up to 3.0 mm shorter than Japanese brome and does not bend backwards from the lemma.  Field brome has glabrous lemmas.
